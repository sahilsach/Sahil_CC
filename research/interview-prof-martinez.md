# Interview Transcript: Dr. Elena Martinez
**Date:** October 15, 2025
**Interviewer:** David (PM)
**Duration:** 47 minutes
**Course:** Introduction to Psychology (PSY 101), 280 students, 6 TAs

---

*[Recording starts mid-conversation, some background noise from coffee shop]*

**David:** ...so yeah, just tell me about your experience. Whatever comes to mind.

**Dr. Martinez:** Where do I even start? Okay so I've been teaching intro psych for twelve years now. Twelve years! And you know what's funny, the content barely changes - Freud is still Freud, cognitive biases are still cognitive biases - but everything about how we manage the course has gotten so much more complicated.

**David:** How so?

**Dr. Martinez:** Well, okay, so when I started we had maybe 80 students and two TAs and we'd all sit in my office and grade together. Like literally sitting at the same table. If someone had a question about a rubric they'd just ask. Now I've got 280 students and six TAs and half of them I only see at the weekly meeting for like twenty minutes.

**David:** That's a big change.

**Dr. Martinez:** It's a totally different job. And I don't think people - like the administration - really gets that. They're like "you've been teaching this class forever, it should be easy by now." And I'm like, no, I'm basically running a small company now. I'm managing six people who are also full-time graduate students with their own research and their own stress and their own lives.

*[pause, sounds like sipping coffee]*

**Dr. Martinez:** Can I tell you my biggest headache? It's not the grading itself. Like, the actual act of reading an essay and assigning a grade - that's fine. I could do that in my sleep. The headache is making sure all six TAs are grading the same way.

**David:** Can you give me an example?

**Dr. Martinez:** Oh god, so many examples. Okay so last semester - this still makes me angry - I had a student come to office hours, really upset, saying she got a C on an essay. And she's like "my roommate wrote almost the same thing and got an A minus." And I'm thinking, okay, students always say that, right? But she actually showed me both essays. Side by side. And honestly? They were really similar quality. Not identical, but you know, close.

**David:** Wow.

**Dr. Martinez:** Yeah. So I looked into it and turns out one was graded by my TA Sarah who's... let's say rigorous. And the other was graded by - I won't say his name but - one of my TAs who I suspect just kind of skims things? And I pull the grade distributions and Sarah's section is averaging like a B minus and this other TA's section is averaging a B plus. For the same assignments. Same rubric.

**David:** How did that happen?

**Dr. Martinez:** That's what I want to know! I mean, I give them all the same rubric. We do a calibration session at the start of semester where we grade sample essays together. But it just... doesn't stick? By week eight everyone's kind of drifted back to their own internal standards. Sarah thinks she's grading correctly and so does the other guy and honestly I don't even know which one is right anymore.

**David:** Do you have a way to track that? Like to see the drift happening?

**Dr. Martinez:** *[laughs]* No! That's the thing. I have no visibility until a student complains or I happen to notice something weird. GradeFlow gives me the grades obviously but it doesn't like... analyze them for me? I can export to Excel and do my own analysis but who has time for that. I've got 280 students and my own research and I'm on like three committees...

*[long pause]*

**Dr. Martinez:** You know what I'd love? This is what I actually need. I need something that just tells me "hey, Sarah is grading way harder than everyone else" BEFORE it becomes a problem. Like an alert. Or even just a dashboard where I can see at a glance - are my TAs converging or diverging?

**David:** That makes sense. What about workload - you mentioned that earlier?

**Dr. Martinez:** Oh that's the other thing. So I have no idea which TAs are struggling until things completely fall apart. Last spring I had a TA - really sweet, really hardworking - and halfway through the semester she just... disappeared. Like stopped responding to emails, grades weren't getting entered. Turns out she had a family emergency and was completely overwhelmed and didn't know how to tell me. She was embarrassed I think?

**David:** That's really hard.

**Dr. Martinez:** And by the time I found out, she was like three weeks behind on grading. Three weeks! Her students were furious. Rightfully! And I had to scramble to redistribute all that work to the other TAs who were already busy. It was a mess.

**David:** Is there anything in GradeFlow that helps with that?

**Dr. Martinez:** I mean, I can see the grades that have been entered. So if I think to check, I can see oh, this person hasn't entered any grades for this assignment. But there's no like... proactive thing? No notification that says "hey, grades are usually done by now and this TA hasn't started." I'm only going to notice if I go looking, and usually I'm too busy to go looking until someone complains.

**David:** So you want earlier warning signs?

**Dr. Martinez:** Yes exactly. Early warning. Both for grading consistency and for workload. Like, tell me when something is drifting off track BEFORE it becomes a crisis that I have to spend my weekend fixing.

*[background noise increases - sounds like more people entering the coffee shop]*

**Dr. Martinez:** Can I tell you something kind of embarrassing?

**David:** Sure, this is all confidential.

**Dr. Martinez:** I spend probably... five hours a week? Just on TA management stuff. Not the teaching, not the content, not the actual grading. Just managing the people who do the grading. Checking in on them, answering their questions, reviewing their work, dealing with grade disputes. That's like a whole day every week that I could be spending on research or actually helping students.

**David:** Five hours is a lot.

**Dr. Martinez:** And honestly that's probably low because I don't count all the mental energy, you know? Like lying in bed on Sunday night thinking "did I check Marcus's grades? Is Emily okay? Why did that student email me?"

*[laughs]*

**Dr. Martinez:** Sorry, this is turning into therapy.

**David:** No, this is really helpful. Can you tell me more about the calibration session you mentioned?

**Dr. Martinez:** Oh sure. So at the start of every semester, before we start grading anything real, I pull together three sample essays - one that's clearly an A, one that's clearly a C, and one that's kind of in the middle, like a B or B minus. And we all grade them independently, then we compare and talk about it. Usually takes like two hours.

**David:** Does it help?

**Dr. Martinez:** It helps a lot! For like... two weeks. Maybe three. Everyone leaves that session on the same page. And then life happens. People get busy with their own research. They forget the nuances we discussed. They develop shortcuts. By midterms we're basically back to square one.

**David:** Have you tried doing it more often?

**Dr. Martinez:** I've thought about it but when would we? Everyone's already stretched thin. Asking my TAs to come in for another two hour calibration session in week eight - they'd mutiny. And honestly I don't have time either.

**David:** What would it take to make calibration more... continuous?

**Dr. Martinez:** Hm. That's a good question. I mean, ideally it wouldn't be a thing I have to facilitate, right? Like if TAs could just see - anonymized or whatever - how their grades compare to other TAs on the same work. Not as a punishment thing but as a learning thing. Like "oh interesting, I gave this a B minus and the average was B plus, let me think about whether I'm being too harsh."

**David:** Self-calibration almost.

**Dr. Martinez:** Yeah! Self-calibration. I like that. Because the problem with me pointing out differences is it feels like criticism. But if the system just shows it neutrally, it's more like information.

*[pause]*

**Dr. Martinez:** You know what GradeFlow reminds me of? It's like... it's like someone built the world's best grade book but forgot that grades come from humans who need support and oversight. The technical stuff is fine. Great even. But the human management layer is missing.

**David:** That's a good way to put it.

**Dr. Martinez:** Like, GradeFlow doesn't solve my real problem. My real problem is making sure that a student's grade doesn't depend on which TA they got assigned to. And right now? It totally does. A student in Sarah's section has to work significantly harder to get an A than a student in [other TA]'s section. That's not fair. That's actually kind of unethical if you think about it.

**David:** That's a strong word.

**Dr. Martinez:** I mean, grades matter! They affect scholarships, grad school applications, self-confidence. If I'm allowing unfair grades because I don't have the tools to ensure consistency, that's on me. I'm failing those students.

*[long pause]*

**Dr. Martinez:** Sorry, I'm getting on a soapbox. Is this the kind of stuff you're looking for?

**David:** This is exactly what I'm looking for. A few more questions - do you use any of the analytics features in GradeFlow currently?

**Dr. Martinez:** The student analytics? Yeah, I love those. I can see who's struggling, who's improving, who's falling behind. It actually helped me identify a student with an undiagnosed learning disability last year because the pattern of grades was so unusual. Referred them to disability services and they're doing much better now.

**David:** That's great.

**Dr. Martinez:** Yeah so I want that same level of insight for my TAs. Not to punish anyone, but to help them. If I can see that a TA is taking twice as long as everyone else, maybe they need more training. If I can see that a TA's grades are outliers, maybe the rubric wasn't clear enough. It's diagnostic, not punitive.

**David:** One last question - if you could wave a magic wand and have GradeFlow do one thing it doesn't do now, what would it be?

**Dr. Martinez:** Oh easy. Show me when my TAs are diverging. Like, a simple chart. "Here's the grade distribution by TA. Sarah is X, Marcus is Y, they're moving apart." And alert me before it becomes a problem I have to spend my weekend fixing.

**David:** Thank you so much, this has been incredibly helpful.

**Dr. Martinez:** Of course! And look, I love GradeFlow, I'm not trying to trash it. It's so much better than what I was using before. I just think there's this whole opportunity around TA management that's kind of... untapped? Like y'all focused on the grading part and forgot about the people doing the grading.

**David:** That's really valuable feedback.

**Dr. Martinez:** Happy to help. Let me know if you need anything else - I'm always available to complain about TA management.

*[laughter, recording ends]*
