# Interview Transcript: James (TA)
**Date:** October 28, 2025
**Interviewer:** David (PM)
**Duration:** 41 minutes
**Course:** Data Structures (CS 201)
**Year:** 1st year PhD student

---

*[Recording starts, sounds like library study room]*

**David:** Thanks for making time. I know first-year PhD students are busy.

**James:** Ha. Busy is an understatement. But yeah, happy to help. I have opinions.

**David:** Great, that's what I want. Tell me about your experience as a TA.

**James:** Okay so, full disclosure - I'm not doing great at this. Like, I'm barely keeping my head above water. This is my first semester TAing ever and I feel like I was thrown into the deep end with no training.

**David:** No training at all?

**James:** I mean, there was like a one-hour orientation at the start of the semester? Where they showed us how to use GradeFlow and where to find the rubrics. But nothing about like, how to actually grade. How to interpret the rubric. What to do when a submission doesn't fit neatly into the categories.

**David:** That sounds frustrating.

**James:** It's terrifying honestly. Like, I'm looking at someone's code and trying to decide how many points to give for partial credit and I have no idea if I'm doing it right. What if I'm being too harsh? What if I'm too lenient? Nobody told me.

**David:** Have you asked for help?

**James:** Kind of? But Professor Chen is super busy and I don't want to seem incompetent. Like, I'm supposed to be this PhD student who knows the material cold - which I do, I know data structures really well - but knowing the material and knowing how to grade are completely different skills.

**David:** That's true.

**James:** And the other TAs have been doing this for years. They just... know. They look at a submission and know immediately what grade it deserves. For me, every submission is like a puzzle I have to figure out.

*[pause]*

**James:** Can I tell you something embarrassing?

**David:** This is all confidential.

**James:** I'm really behind on grading. Like, really behind. I haven't told Professor Chen because I'm hoping I can catch up, but I'm probably... forty hours behind? Maybe more?

**David:** That's a lot. How did that happen?

**James:** I just... got overwhelmed. The first assignment took me way longer than I expected because I was being really careful. Then I fell a little behind. Then the second assignment came in before I finished the first. And it just snowballed from there.

**David:** Does Professor Chen know?

**James:** No. And I really don't want him to find out because it'll look bad for my TA evaluation. And I need this funding - it's paying for my PhD. So I'm just... working nights and weekends trying to catch up.

**David:** That sounds stressful.

**James:** It's brutal. Like, I'm supposed to be doing research and taking classes and TAing and I'm falling behind on all three because the grading is taking all my time. But I can't tell anyone because asking for help feels like admitting failure.

*[long pause, sound of typing]*

**James:** Sorry, I'm looking at my to-do list. It's depressing.

**David:** No worries. Can you tell me more about what makes grading take so long?

**James:** Okay so with CS it's not like grading essays where you read it once and form an opinion. You have to actually run the code, see if it works, understand why it doesn't work if it fails, figure out what the student was trying to do, decide how much partial credit to give...

**David:** And the rubric doesn't help?

**James:** The rubric is like... guidelines? It says stuff like "deduct 2 points for logical errors" but what counts as a logical error? Is an off-by-one bug a logical error or an implementation error? Different TAs would answer that differently.

**David:** So there's room for interpretation.

**James:** Tons of room. And I end up second-guessing myself constantly. Like, I'll grade something, give it a 7/10, and then think "wait, was that too harsh? Should it be an 8?" And then I'll go back and look at it again and waste another five minutes.

**David:** Does that happen a lot?

**James:** All the time. Some assignments I've graded the same submission like three times because I keep changing my mind. Which is probably why I'm so behind.

*[pause]*

**James:** What would really help is if I could see how other TAs graded similar submissions. Like, not to copy them, but just to calibrate. "Oh, they gave this kind of error a 2-point deduction, okay, that's the standard." Right now I'm just guessing.

**David:** You can't see how others grade currently?

**James:** No, everyone grades in isolation. I have no idea if my grades are aligned with the rest of the team. For all I know I'm the hardest grader or the easiest. I genuinely have no clue.

**David:** What about asking the experienced TAs?

**James:** I've done that a little but I feel bad taking up their time. They have their own work. And honestly, some of them are kind of intimidating? Like they've been doing this forever and I'm the clueless new person asking obvious questions.

**David:** That's understandable.

**James:** I wish there was like... a mentorship system? Or at least examples. Like, "here's a submission that got full credit, here's one that got 70%, here's one that got 50%." Show me the spectrum so I know where to place things.

*[background noise - door opening]*

**James:** Oh that's probably my research meeting, I might need to wrap up soon.

**David:** Sure. One quick question - if you could change one thing about the TA experience, what would it be?

**James:** Honestly? Better training. Or not even training - just... documentation? Examples? Something that helps me know if I'm grading correctly before I submit grades. Right now I submit grades and just pray I didn't mess up. And then sometimes a student appeals and Professor Chen agrees with them and I feel stupid.

**David:** That happens?

**James:** Yeah, a couple times. The appeal process is basically "student complains, professor reviews, professor overrules my grade." Which is fine, I guess I was wrong. But nobody tells me why I was wrong so I just make the same mistakes again.

**David:** So no feedback loop.

**James:** Zero. I'm flying blind, making mistakes, not learning from them. It's a terrible system.

**David:** Thank you James, this has been really valuable.

**James:** Sure. And look, I'm not trying to complain - I chose to be a TA and it's paying for my PhD. But there's got to be a better way to support new TAs. Right now it feels like sink or swim and I'm barely swimming.

*[recording ends]*
