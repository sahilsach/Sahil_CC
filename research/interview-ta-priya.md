# Interview Transcript: Priya (TA)
**Date:** October 30, 2025
**Interviewer:** David (PM)
**Duration:** 33 minutes
**Course:** Organic Chemistry (CHEM 301)
**Year:** 3rd year PhD student

---

*[Recording starts, sounds like chemistry building hallway, echoes]*

**David:** Thanks for grabbing a few minutes. I know you're busy with lab.

**Priya:** No problem, I needed a break anyway. Been in there since 7am. What do you want to know?

**David:** Just your experience as a TA, especially around grading. Whatever comes to mind.

**Priya:** Okay. So I've been TAing for Dr. Okonkwo for two years now. Orgo, which is... intense. The students are stressed, the material is hard, and there's a lot of grading because it's problem sets plus labs plus exams.

**David:** How's the grading part?

**Priya:** Honestly? Fine for me personally. I'm fast. I've been doing this long enough that I can look at a problem set and know immediately what grade it deserves. Like, pattern recognition at this point.

**David:** How long does it take you?

**Priya:** Maybe 3-4 minutes per problem set? Sometimes less. I can do like 25 an hour if I'm in the zone.

**David:** That's impressive.

**Priya:** It's just practice. The first semester I was slow too. But you learn the common mistakes, the common approaches, and it speeds up.

*[pause]*

**Priya:** The problem is, not everyone is at that level. And there's no way to share what I know.

**David:** What do you mean?

**Priya:** Like, the new TAs are struggling. I can see it. They're grading the same problem sets I am and it takes them three times as long. And they're probably making mistakes because they don't know the patterns yet. But there's no mechanism for experienced TAs to help new ones.

**David:** You can't just... tell them?

**Priya:** I mean, I try to? If someone asks me a question in the TA room, I'll help. But most of them don't ask. Either they're embarrassed or they're too busy or they don't know they need help. And I'm not their supervisor, I can't force them to listen to me.

**David:** So knowledge stays siloed.

**Priya:** Exactly. Everyone figures out their own system independently. Which is wildly inefficient. I've developed all these mental shortcuts over two years and they'll probably disappear when I graduate.

**David:** That's a loss.

**Priya:** Right? Dr. Okonkwo has me informally mentoring the new TAs but it's just... ad hoc. Random Slack messages. It's not a system.

*[sound of footsteps passing]*

**Priya:** Actually, can I tell you something else that bugs me?

**David:** Please.

**Priya:** So I'm fast and accurate at grading. I think I do a really good job. But nobody knows. Nobody recognizes it. Like, there's no way for Dr. Okonkwo to see "Priya finishes her grading in half the time and with fewer complaints." I'm just... invisible.

**David:** You want recognition.

**Priya:** Not like a trophy or anything. But just - acknowledgment? And maybe the ability to help more formally. Like, if I'm consistently the best grader, maybe I should be training the new people. But there's no data to show that's true.

**David:** GradeFlow doesn't track that?

**Priya:** I don't think so? I mean, it tracks when grades are submitted but not like... grading quality or efficiency. So Dr. Okonkwo doesn't have objective data on who's doing well and who's struggling.

**David:** What would you want to see tracked?

**Priya:** Time per submission is obvious. Grade distribution compared to average - like, are you an outlier? Number of appeals or grade changes - if you're getting overruled a lot, that's a signal. And maybe feedback quality, though that's harder to measure.

**David:** That makes sense.

**Priya:** Because right now it's all vibes. Dr. Okonkwo thinks I'm a good TA because I never complain and students don't complain about me. But she's not actually measuring anything. And that means inefficient TAs can fly under the radar.

*[pause]*

**Priya:** Oh, and here's another thing - the rubric interpretation problem.

**David:** Tell me about that.

**Priya:** So the rubric will say something like "2 points for correct approach." What's a correct approach? If a student gets the right answer but skips a step, is that correct? If they show the step but make an arithmetic error, is that approach correct but execution wrong?

**David:** Those seem like reasonable questions.

**Priya:** They come up constantly! And different TAs answer them differently. I've developed my own internal rules over time - "arithmetic errors are half credit, conceptual errors are no credit" - but a new TA wouldn't know that.

**David:** Why not write it down?

**Priya:** I should. But nobody asked me to. There's no place for it in GradeFlow. The rubric is what Dr. Okonkwo creates at the start of semester and it doesn't evolve based on the edge cases we encounter.

**David:** An evolving rubric would help?

**Priya:** Massively. Like, the first time someone asks "how do I grade a sign error?" we make a decision and record it. Then every future TA knows. Instead of rediscovering the same questions every semester.

**David:** That's a good idea.

**Priya:** It's just... basic knowledge management? But academia doesn't do that. Every generation of TAs reinvents the wheel.

*[phone buzzes]*

**Priya:** Oh, I need to get back. My reaction is probably done.

**David:** Of course. Any final thoughts?

**Priya:** Just that - I actually like TAing. I'm good at it. But the system doesn't recognize good TAing or help struggling TAs improve. It's just... everyone doing their own thing and hoping it works out. Which usually it does, but not optimally.

**David:** Thanks so much Priya.

**Priya:** Sure. Let me know if you build something cool.

*[recording ends]*
