# Interview Transcript: Marcus (TA)
**Date:** October 30, 2025
**Interviewer:** David (PM)
**Duration:** 29 minutes
**Course:** American History (HIST 201)
**Year:** 2nd year MA student

---

*[Recording starts, sounds like outdoor campus area, birds chirping]*

**David:** Thanks for meeting me out here. Nice day for it.

**Marcus:** Yeah, I needed some fresh air. Been grading all morning. Fifty essays don't read themselves.

**David:** Speaking of which - tell me about your experience with grading.

**Marcus:** What do you want to know? I grade stuff, students complain about their grades, I explain why I graded it that way, cycle repeats.

**David:** *[laughs]* That sounds cynical.

**Marcus:** Maybe a little. I don't know, I've been TAing for a year and a half now and I think I've developed a certain... pragmatism. Like, you can't spend twenty minutes on every essay. There aren't enough hours in the day.

**David:** How long do you spend per essay?

**Marcus:** Five, six minutes? Depends on the length. Shorter ones maybe three or four.

**David:** That's efficient.

**Marcus:** I call it efficient, Professor Williams calls it "rushed." Same thing I guess.

**David:** Have you gotten feedback that you're rushing?

**Marcus:** Sort of. I got dinged in my TA evaluation last semester for "insufficient feedback." Students were complaining that my comments weren't detailed enough. Meanwhile Emily - the other TA - is writing like paragraphs on every paper and I'm like... who has time for that?

*[pause]*

**Marcus:** Don't get me wrong, Emily's feedback is great. She's really thorough. But she's also always behind and stressed out and taking work home on weekends. That's not sustainable either.

**David:** So you're faster but getting complaints. She's slower but getting praised.

**Marcus:** Basically yeah. And I'm not sure who's right. Maybe my feedback is too sparse. Or maybe she's overinvesting and I'm at the appropriate level. Nobody's actually defined what "sufficient feedback" looks like.

**David:** What would help with that?

**Marcus:** Examples! Like, "here's a comment that's too short, here's one that's too long, here's the goldilocks zone." Show me the target so I can aim at it. Right now I'm just guessing.

**David:** Do you have that conversation with Professor Williams?

**Marcus:** Not really. He's busy and I don't want to bother him with "teach me how to write comments." Seems like something I should figure out on my own. But clearly I haven't figured it out since students are complaining.

*[sound of bicycle passing]*

**Marcus:** Can I vent about something else?

**David:** Go ahead.

**Marcus:** Okay so the other thing that frustrates me is - I have no idea how my grades compare to Emily's. Like, we split the class alphabetically. She grades A through L, I grade M through Z. We never grade the same essay so there's no way to compare.

**David:** Why does that matter?

**Marcus:** Because what if I'm way harsher than her? Or way easier? Those students are getting systematically different treatment based on where their last name falls in the alphabet. That's not fair.

**David:** Have you tried to compare?

**Marcus:** Professor Williams did this thing last semester where he gave us both the same essay to grade without telling us. Emily gave it a B+. I gave it a C+. Full letter grade difference.

**David:** Wow.

**Marcus:** Yeah. So clearly we're not calibrated. But then what? He told us both to "be more aligned" but how? It's not like I'm trying to grade differently. I think my grades are right. Emily thinks her grades are right. Who's actually right?

**David:** Did you resolve it?

**Marcus:** Not really. We did a calibration session, talked about it, agreed we'd try to be more similar. But without ongoing comparison, how would we know if it worked? We probably just drifted back to our natural tendencies.

**David:** That's frustrating.

**Marcus:** Very. And I feel bad for the students honestly. They signed up for HIST 201, not "lottery where your grade depends on which TA you get."

*[pause]*

**Marcus:** You know what I'd want? Real-time feedback on my grading patterns. Like, "Marcus, your average grade is X, Emily's is Y, here's the gap." Let me see it as it develops so I can adjust.

**David:** Self-correction basically.

**Marcus:** Yeah. Right now I only find out about problems retroactively. Student complains, Professor Williams investigates, turns out there's an issue. By then I've already graded dozens of papers with that bias.

**David:** Makes sense.

**Marcus:** And the thing is - I want to do a good job. I'm not cutting corners because I don't care. I'm cutting corners because nobody told me how much corner-cutting is acceptable. If the standard is 15 minutes per essay, okay, I'll spend 15 minutes. But I assumed 5 was fine and apparently it's not.

**David:** Expectations weren't clear.

**Marcus:** Exactly. I'm playing a game where nobody told me the rules, and now I'm losing points for breaking rules I didn't know existed.

**David:** That's a great way to put it.

**Marcus:** It's just frustrating. I'm trying to be efficient, get through my workload, have time for my own thesis. But apparently efficiency looks like laziness. Or maybe it is laziness and I need to step up. I genuinely don't know!

*[long pause]*

**David:** Any other thoughts before we wrap up?

**Marcus:** Just that... I think TAs are kind of invisible in the system? Like, professors have all this support - peer review, teaching evaluations, mentorship. TAs get thrown into grading with minimal guidance and minimal feedback. We're just supposed to figure it out.

**David:** That resonates with what I've heard from others.

**Marcus:** Good. Maybe that means something will change. Because right now it feels like everyone's struggling in isolation.

**David:** Thank you Marcus, really helpful.

**Marcus:** Sure. Good luck with whatever you're building.

*[recording ends]*
