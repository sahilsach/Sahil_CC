# Interview Transcript: Dr. James Williams
**Date:** October 25, 2025
**Interviewer:** David (PM)
**Duration:** 38 minutes
**Course:** American History (HIST 201), 95 students, 2 TAs

---

*[Recording starts, professor's office, papers shuffling]*

**Dr. Williams:** Sorry, just moving some things... okay. Where should I start?

**David:** Wherever you'd like. Just tell me about your experience.

**Dr. Williams:** Alright well, I should say upfront - I'm kind of a dinosaur. I resisted these digital grading tools for years. YEARS. I was perfectly happy with paper essays and red pens. My colleagues kept telling me "James, you have to try GradeFlow" and I kept saying "I'm fine, thanks."

**David:** What changed?

**Dr. Williams:** Honestly? The pandemic. Suddenly everything had to be online and I couldn't have students handing me physical papers anymore. So I finally tried GradeFlow and - I'll admit this - it was better than I expected. For the assignment management part anyway. Having everything in one place, being able to annotate digitally, students getting their feedback faster. That's all good.

**David:** What about the TA management part?

**Dr. Williams:** That's where I'm less impressed. And I don't have a huge operation - just two TAs for about 95 students. But even with two people, I have consistency issues.

**David:** Can you tell me about those?

**Dr. Williams:** Yeah so. Essay grading is inherently subjective. I accept that. Unlike math or chemistry where there's a right answer, with history essays you're evaluating arguments, evidence, writing quality, originality. Reasonable people can disagree.

**David:** That makes sense.

**Dr. Williams:** But - and this is important - my two TAs should not be a full letter grade apart on the same essay. That's not subjectivity, that's a problem. And I've seen it happen.

**David:** A full letter grade?

**Dr. Williams:** Yep. I did an experiment last semester. Took an essay, made two copies, gave one to each TA without telling them. Emily gave it a B+. Marcus gave it a C+. Same essay!

**David:** That's significant.

**Dr. Williams:** It's huge. And if that's happening on my little test, it's happening all the time on real assignments. Students in Emily's half of the alphabet are getting systematically higher grades than students in Marcus's half. That's not fair.

**David:** How do you split the work?

**Dr. Williams:** Alphabetically. Emily grades A through L, Marcus grades M through Z. It's simple to administer but it means students only ever get graded by one TA. So the inconsistency directly affects their final grades.

**David:** Could you change the system?

**Dr. Williams:** I've thought about it. Like having each TA grade half of each assignment randomly. But that's more complicated to manage and... honestly I'm not sure it would help. They'd still be inconsistent, it would just be distributed differently.

**David:** What about calibration?

**Dr. Williams:** We do it. At the start of every semester, I have both TAs grade three sample essays and then we compare. Usually takes a couple hours. And it helps - for a while.

**David:** How long does it last?

**Dr. Williams:** Couple weeks, maybe a month. Then everyone drifts. By midterms we're back to having significant differences. The calibration wears off.

**David:** Why do you think that happens?

**Dr. Williams:** Because grading essays is exhausting and everyone develops shortcuts. You start out being really careful and thorough, applying every rubric criterion. By week six you're skimming and going with your gut. And different people's guts give different answers.

*[pause]*

**Dr. Williams:** Can I tell you something else I discovered?

**David:** Please.

**Dr. Williams:** I looked at grading time. Like, how long each TA spent on each essay. GradeFlow tracks this I think? Or I had them log it, I don't remember. Anyway, Marcus was averaging about 5-6 minutes per essay. Emily was averaging 15-20 minutes.

**David:** That's a big difference.

**Dr. Williams:** Huge difference! And when I looked at the feedback quality - not the grades, the actual comments - it was night and day. Emily's comments were detailed, constructive, referenced specific passages. Marcus's comments were like "Good argument but needs more evidence." Three sentences max.

**David:** Were students noticing?

**Dr. Williams:** Oh yeah. I had students literally asking to switch sections because they'd heard Emily gives better feedback. Which is awkward. And probably not allowed for fairness reasons.

**David:** So you have a quality vs speed problem too.

**Dr. Williams:** Right. And I don't really know what to do about it. Is Marcus doing it wrong? Or is Emily spending too much time and the 5 minute approach is actually fine? I don't have a standard to compare against.

**David:** What would help?

**Dr. Williams:** I keep thinking about examples. Like, "here's what good feedback looks like" and "here's what's too sparse." Not just telling TAs to write better comments but showing them. But creating those examples is a lot of work and I don't have time.

**David:** Would it help if GradeFlow had feedback templates or examples?

**Dr. Williams:** Maybe? I'd have to see how it worked. The problem is essay feedback is so context-specific. You can't just copy-paste the same comment on every paper. But maybe like... starter phrases? Common patterns? I don't know.

*[long pause]*

**Dr. Williams:** You know what I really want? I want ongoing calibration that doesn't require me to organize it.

**David:** What would that look like?

**Dr. Williams:** Like, what if every week, both TAs graded one or two of the same essays - randomly selected - and then could see how the other person graded them? Not to argue about it but just to stay calibrated. Keeps them aware of each other's standards without me having to facilitate.

**David:** Automatic calibration basically.

**Dr. Williams:** Yes! Automatic. Continuous. Background. I don't want to have to schedule calibration sessions or analyze spreadsheets. I want the system to do it for me and just tell me if there's a problem.

**David:** That makes a lot of sense.

**Dr. Williams:** Because right now I'm kind of flying blind. I know there's inconsistency - I've proven it to myself - but I don't have real-time visibility into how bad it is or whether it's getting better or worse. I only find out when a student complains or when I do my own investigation.

**David:** Is there anything in GradeFlow that helps with this currently?

**Dr. Williams:** Not really. I can see grades obviously but there's no comparison view. No analytics by TA. Nothing that says "hey, your TAs are diverging." I have to go looking for problems, and I usually don't have time to look.

*[papers shuffling]*

**Dr. Williams:** I should wrap up soon - I have office hours starting.

**David:** Of course. One quick last question - what's the number one thing that would make your life easier?

**Dr. Williams:** Hmm. Probably grade comparison by TA. Like, one chart showing "Emily's average is X, Marcus's average is Y, here's how they've trended over the semester." Right now I have to export to Excel and make that chart myself. If GradeFlow just showed it to me, I'd be much more likely to catch problems early.

**David:** Thank you so much, Dr. Williams. This has been really helpful.

**Dr. Williams:** Happy to help. And like I said, I was a skeptic who got converted. GradeFlow is good at what it does. I just think there's room to do more for those of us managing TAs.

**David:** Absolutely. Thanks again.

*[recording ends]*
