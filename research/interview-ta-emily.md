# Interview Transcript: Emily (TA)
**Date:** November 1, 2025
**Interviewer:** David (PM)
**Duration:** 37 minutes
**Course:** American History (HIST 201)
**Year:** 3rd year PhD student

---

*[Recording starts, sounds like small office, door closing]*

**David:** Thanks for meeting with me. I appreciate you making time.

**Emily:** Of course. Anything to procrastinate on grading. *[laughs]* I'm kidding. Sort of.

**David:** How much grading do you have right now?

**Emily:** About thirty-five essays waiting for me. Plus a stack of reading responses. It never ends.

**David:** That's a lot. Tell me about your experience as a TA.

**Emily:** Okay so, I've been TAing for Professor Williams for two years now. American History, which is mostly essays and short responses. Very writing-intensive. Which means very grading-intensive.

**David:** How do you approach the grading?

**Emily:** Thoroughly. Maybe too thoroughly? I don't know. I believe that feedback is the whole point of grading. Like, a grade without explanation doesn't help students learn. So I write detailed comments on every essay. I reference specific passages, I explain why something works or doesn't work, I suggest how they could improve.

**David:** That sounds valuable.

**Emily:** Students seem to appreciate it. I get really good feedback in course evaluations. "Emily's comments helped me become a better writer." That kind of thing. It feels good.

**David:** So what's the problem?

**Emily:** The problem is it takes forever. *[sighs]* I spend like 15-20 minutes per essay. Sometimes more for students who are really struggling. Marcus - the other TA - does it in five minutes.

**David:** That's quite a difference.

**Emily:** Yeah. And he finishes his grading in like a day. I take a week. Sometimes more. I'm perpetually behind.

*[pause]*

**Emily:** And here's the thing - I don't think I'm doing it wrong? Like, the quality of my feedback is objectively higher. But nobody rewards that. There's no "good feedback" bonus. I'm just spending more time for no extra recognition while Marcus zips through and has free time.

**David:** Does Professor Williams notice?

**Emily:** He notices I'm slower. I don't think he realizes why. Or maybe he does and doesn't care? I don't know. We've never had a direct conversation about it.

**David:** Would it help to have that conversation?

**Emily:** Maybe. But what would I say? "Hey, I'm doing a better job than Marcus but it takes me longer, can you tell him to try harder?" That seems petty. And maybe my standard is too high. Maybe Marcus's five-minute approach is actually sufficient and I'm overinvesting.

**David:** How do you know what the right level is?

**Emily:** I don't! That's the frustrating part. There's no benchmark. No example that says "this is what good feedback looks like." I just do what feels right and hope it's appreciated.

*[long pause]*

**David:** What happens when you fall behind?

**Emily:** Students get annoyed. They email asking where their grades are. Professor Williams gets copied on those emails. It's embarrassing.

**David:** Can you get help when you're behind?

**Emily:** Not really. Like, there's no mechanism for it. I can't just ask Marcus to take some of my essays - they're alphabetically assigned. And I don't want to admit I can't keep up because that feels like failure.

**David:** You mentioned you had to take a mental health day last month...

**Emily:** Yeah. I just... hit a wall. Couldn't face another essay. Took a day off. And of course that put me further behind which made everything worse. Vicious cycle.

**David:** What would have helped in that moment?

**Emily:** Honestly? If the system had noticed I was struggling before I crashed. Or if there was a way to flag "I need help" without it feeling like a big deal. Like, a button I could click that says "redistribute some of my work to available TAs." No shame, no conversation, just... help.

**David:** That's interesting.

**Emily:** Because the current system assumes everyone is always fine and working at 100% capacity. But people aren't fine! They have bad weeks, bad days, personal stuff going on. And there's no flexibility. You're either keeping up or you're not, and if you're not, you suffer in silence.

*[sound of papers shuffling]*

**Emily:** Can I tell you what really bothers me?

**David:** Please.

**Emily:** The grading inconsistency. Between me and Marcus. We split the class alphabetically, right? So students A-L get graded by me, M-Z by Marcus. And I'm pretty sure my grades are systematically lower than his.

**David:** How do you know?

**Emily:** I've seen snippets. Professor Williams did a test where he gave us the same essay - I gave it a B+, Marcus gave it a C+. Full letter grade different.

**David:** That's significant.

**Emily:** Right? So either I'm too hard or he's too easy. And we've never resolved which. We did a calibration session, talked about it, tried to align. But without ongoing comparison, how do we know if it stuck? We don't grade the same essays, so we can't compare in real-time.

**David:** What would help?

**Emily:** Maybe some kind of shared calibration? Like, once a week we both grade the same essay and see if we agree. Or the system shows us our grade distributions and highlights when we're diverging. Something to keep us honest.

**David:** Self-correction.

**Emily:** Exactly. Because I'm not trying to grade differently than Marcus. I genuinely think my grades are appropriate. He probably thinks the same. We're both operating in good faith, just with different standards. And without feedback, we'll never converge.

*[pause]*

**David:** What about the workload visibility issue?

**Emily:** What do you mean?

**David:** Like, you mentioned there's no way to flag when you're struggling. Does Professor Williams have visibility into your progress?

**Emily:** Sort of? He can see which grades have been entered. But he can't see that I'm drowning emotionally, or that I stayed up until 2am grading, or that I'm cutting corners on my own research because TAing is eating all my time. That's invisible.

**David:** Would you want him to know?

**Emily:** Part of me says yes, because then he could help. But part of me says no, because it might affect my TA evaluation or make him think I can't handle it. Which I can handle it - just barely. But the system isn't designed to support TAs who actually care about quality.

**David:** That's a strong statement.

**Emily:** It's true though. The system rewards speed. Get your grades in fast, don't make waves, move on. There's no reward for taking the time to provide meaningful feedback. And that's backwards, because feedback is how students learn.

*[long pause]*

**David:** Any final thoughts?

**Emily:** Just that - I love teaching. I love helping students improve. That's why I spend 20 minutes per essay instead of 5. But the system doesn't value that. The system just wants grades entered. And eventually I'll probably burn out and become another Marcus, zipping through essays in five minutes with generic comments. Because that's what the incentives encourage.

**David:** That's a sad prediction.

**Emily:** It's realistic. You can only swim against the current for so long.

**David:** Thank you Emily. This has been really valuable.

**Emily:** Sure. I hope something comes of it. TAs need better support.

*[recording ends]*
