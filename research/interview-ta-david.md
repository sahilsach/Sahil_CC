# Interview Transcript: David K. (TA)
**Date:** November 4, 2025
**Interviewer:** David (PM)
**Duration:** 31 minutes
**Course:** Organic Chemistry (CHEM 301)
**Year:** 1st year PhD student

---

*[Recording starts, sounds like quiet study area]*

**David PM:** Thanks for meeting with me. And just to clarify for the recording - you're David, I'm also David, this might get confusing.

**David K.:** *[laughs]* Yeah, I thought about that. Just call me David K. I'll answer to it.

**David PM:** Perfect. So tell me about your experience as a TA.

**David K.:** Honestly? It's terrifying. This is my first semester ever TAing. I just started my PhD in August and suddenly I'm responsible for grading problem sets for 180 students. That's a lot of trust to put in someone who has no idea what they're doing.

**David PM:** No training?

**David K.:** There was an orientation. Like two hours? They showed us how to use GradeFlow, where to find the rubrics, how to submit grades. But nothing about how to actually grade. How to interpret the rubric. How to handle edge cases. All the stuff that actually matters.

**David PM:** So you were thrown into the deep end.

**David K.:** Completely. And everyone else - the other TAs - they've been doing this for years. They look at a problem and instantly know what it's worth. For me, every single problem is like a puzzle I have to solve.

*[pause]*

**David K.:** Can I be honest about something?

**David PM:** Please, that's what I'm here for.

**David K.:** I'm slow. Like, really slow. The other TAs finish their grading in a couple hours. It takes me all day. Sometimes multiple days. And I know I'm supposed to be faster but I'm so afraid of making mistakes that I double-check everything.

**David PM:** Why are you afraid of mistakes?

**David K.:** Because what if I take off points for something that's actually correct? What if I give credit for something that's wrong? These are students' grades. They matter. They affect GPAs, scholarships, grad school applications. I don't want to mess that up.

**David PM:** That's a lot of pressure for a first-semester TA.

**David K.:** Tell me about it. And the worst part is I have no way to validate myself. I don't know if my grades are right or wrong until a student appeals. And then sometimes Dr. Okonkwo agrees with me and sometimes she doesn't. When she doesn't, I feel terrible.

**David PM:** Does she explain her reasoning?

**David K.:** Not really. She just fixes the grade. So I learn that I was wrong but not why. Which means I probably make the same mistake again.

*[long pause]*

**David K.:** Actually, I have an example. Last week, I looked at how Priya graded a similar problem - Priya's one of the senior TAs, she's been doing this forever - and her grade was completely different from mine. Like, fundamentally different interpretation of the rubric.

**David PM:** What did you do?

**David K.:** Nothing. What could I do? I already submitted my grades. And I'm not going to go to Dr. Okonkwo and say "hey, I think I graded wrong" because that makes me look incompetent. So I just... moved on and hoped nobody noticed.

**David PM:** But you're still not sure who was right?

**David K.:** Exactly. Maybe I was right and Priya was wrong. Maybe she was right and I was wrong. Maybe we were both wrong in different ways. I have no idea. There's no answer key for edge cases.

**David PM:** What would help?

**David K.:** Examples. Please, examples. Just show me what each grade level looks like. "This solution is an A because..." and "This solution is a B because..." and "This is a C because..." Let me learn from concrete instances instead of abstract rubric criteria.

**David PM:** That doesn't exist?

**David K.:** No. The rubric is like "correct approach: 3 points" but what's a correct approach? If the student gets the right answer but skips a step, is the approach correct? Different TAs would answer differently. And there's no document that clarifies.

**David PM:** So everyone develops their own interpretation.

**David K.:** Right. And it probably converges over time as you get more experience. But for a new TA like me, I'm just guessing. Every single grade is a guess.

*[sound of people walking by]*

**David K.:** The other thing that's hard is imposter syndrome. Like, everyone else seems to know what they're doing. They're confident. They finish quickly. And I'm over here taking forever, second-guessing myself, feeling like I don't belong.

**David PM:** Have you talked to anyone about that?

**David K.:** Not really. Who would I talk to? The other TAs are peers, not mentors. Dr. Okonkwo is too busy. There's no formal support system. You're just supposed to figure it out.

**David PM:** What would help with that?

**David K.:** Mentorship would be huge. Like, pairing new TAs with experienced ones. Not just "ask if you have questions" but actual structured support. "We're going to grade these problems together so you can learn my thought process."

**David PM:** Does that happen informally?

**David K.:** Barely. Priya's been helpful when I've reached out, but she has her own work. I feel bad taking her time. And I don't want to seem needy or incompetent.

**David PM:** That seems like a theme - not wanting to ask for help.

**David K.:** It's the academic culture, right? Sink or swim. Figure it out yourself. Asking for help is admitting weakness. Which is toxic but it's real.

*[long pause]*

**David K.:** One more thing - I wish there was some way to check my work before I submit. Like, a sanity check. "Hey, your grades are way lower than the average, you might want to review." Give me a chance to catch issues before they become student complaints.

**David PM:** Pre-submission validation.

**David K.:** Yes! Because once grades are in, they're visible to students. Appeals start. It's a whole thing. But if I knew beforehand that I was an outlier, I could fix it quietly.

**David PM:** That makes a lot of sense.

**David K.:** Right now it's just: grade, submit, pray. That's not a professional system. That's chaos with extra steps.

**David PM:** *[laughs]* That's a good way to put it.

**David K.:** I'm funny when I'm stressed. Which is always, lately.

**David PM:** Anything else you want to share?

**David K.:** Just that... I want to be a good TA. I really do. But the system doesn't set me up to succeed. It assumes I know what I'm doing when I don't. It gives me rubrics without examples. It expects speed without training. And when I mess up, nobody teaches me why. I'm basically learning by failing, which is the worst possible pedagogy for something that affects students' grades.

**David PM:** Thank you David K., this has been incredibly helpful.

**David K.:** Sure. And hey, if you build something that helps new TAs not feel completely lost, I'll be your biggest fan.

**David PM:** I'll keep that in mind.

*[recording ends]*
