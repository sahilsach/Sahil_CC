# Interview Transcript: Sarah (TA)
**Date:** October 28, 2025
**Interviewer:** David (PM)
**Duration:** 35 minutes
**Course:** Introduction to Psychology (PSY 101)
**Year:** 2nd year PhD student

---

*[Recording starts, sounds like campus café, background chatter]*

**David:** Thanks for meeting with me. Coffee's on the company.

**Sarah:** *[laughs]* I'll take any free coffee I can get. PhD life, you know.

**David:** I can imagine. So tell me about your experience as a TA, particularly with GradeFlow.

**Sarah:** Okay so, where to start. I've been TAing for Professor Martinez for three semesters now. Intro psych, which is like the biggest class in the department. 280 students, six TAs. It's a lot.

**David:** That is a lot.

**Sarah:** Yeah. And like, I actually love teaching? Like the office hours and the discussion sections and helping students understand concepts - that's the good part. The grading is... less fun. But necessary I guess.

**David:** What's less fun about it?

**Sarah:** I mean, it's just tedious? And also stressful because I'm never sure if I'm doing it right. Like, there's this constant low-grade anxiety that I'm grading too hard or too easy and I just don't know.

**David:** Can you tell me more about that?

**Sarah:** Okay so here's the thing. I know I grade harder than some of the other TAs. I can just tell from conversations. Like, one of the other TAs - I won't say who - will be like "yeah I gave everyone basically a B or above" and I'm thinking, I gave out multiple Cs and a D. Are we grading the same assignments?

**David:** That's a big difference.

**Sarah:** Right? And I think I'm grading correctly. Like, the rubric says "A requires sophisticated argument with strong evidence" and most of these essays don't have that? They're fine but they're not A work. But maybe the other TA is interpreting "sophisticated" differently than me.

**David:** How do you handle that uncertainty?

**Sarah:** Honestly? I just... live with it. I grade how I think is right and hope for the best. What else can I do? It's not like anyone tells me if I'm on track or not.

*[pause, sounds of drinks being set down]*

**Sarah:** Actually can I tell you something that really bothers me?

**David:** Please.

**Sarah:** So the students have figured out which TAs are easier. Like, there's literally a GroupMe or whatever where they share this info. "Get into section 3, that TA is an easy grader." And I know I'm not one of the "recommended" TAs because I'm "tough but fair" which is code for "will actually give you a B if that's what you earned."

**David:** How does that make you feel?

**Sarah:** Honestly? Kind of demoralized? Like I'm trying to maintain standards and hold students accountable and some of my colleagues are just handing out As. And the students prefer them. Of course they do. Who wouldn't prefer the easy grader?

**David:** Have you talked to Professor Martinez about this?

**Sarah:** Not directly. I mean, what would I say? "Hey, I think the other TAs are too easy"? That sounds so petty. And maybe I'm wrong. Maybe I'm the problem. Maybe my standards are too high.

**David:** So you're operating without feedback.

**Sarah:** Basically yes. I have no idea how I'm doing compared to anyone else. I'm just grading in a vacuum and assuming I'm doing a good job because no one's complained. Well, except students who want higher grades, but they always complain.

*[long pause]*

**Sarah:** You know what I'd really love? This is going to sound weird but - I'd love to see how other TAs graded the same essay I graded. Like, anonymized or whatever. Just to calibrate. Did they give this essay a B+ too? Or did they give it an A-? Am I the outlier or are they?

**David:** That would help?

**Sarah:** So much! Because right now I have no reference point. I'm just guessing. And that feels unprofessional? Like, I'm assigning grades that affect students' GPAs and I genuinely don't know if I'm being fair.

**David:** What about the calibration sessions at the start of semester?

**Sarah:** Oh those are helpful, for sure. But they're like... one moment in time? We grade three sample essays together and agree on what they deserve. And then two weeks later I'm back to grading on my own and I've already forgotten the nuances we discussed.

**David:** So the calibration doesn't stick.

**Sarah:** No. And honestly I think everyone's grades drift over the semester. You start out being really careful and by the end you're just trying to get through the pile. Which is bad, I know, but there's only so many hours in the day.

**David:** How many hours do you spend grading?

**Sarah:** It varies. Maybe... 10-15 hours a week during peak times? Like when the midterm essays come in. Some weeks it's less. But it's unpredictable which makes it hard to plan my actual research work.

*[pause]*

**Sarah:** Can I vent about something else?

**David:** Go ahead.

**Sarah:** Okay so. The rubric. The rubric is supposed to help us grade consistently, right? But it's so vague. Like, one of the criteria is "demonstrates understanding of key concepts." Okay, what does that mean? What does "demonstrates" look like? What counts as "understanding"? Two TAs can read that criterion and apply it completely differently.

**David:** Have you asked for clarification?

**Sarah:** Sort of? But Professor Martinez is so busy. She's great and super approachable but she has 280 students and her own research and like five committees. I feel bad bothering her with rubric questions when she's dealing with bigger things.

**David:** What would make the rubric more useful?

**Sarah:** Examples. Like, "here's what an A essay looks like" and "here's what a B essay looks like." Not just abstract criteria but concrete examples. Show me the thing.

*[background noise increases]*

**Sarah:** Sorry, it's getting loud in here.

**David:** We can wrap up soon. Any last thoughts on what would help you as a TA?

**Sarah:** I think... honestly? Just some feedback. Some sense of whether I'm doing this right. Right now I'm grading hundreds of essays a semester with zero validation that my grades are appropriate. That's kind of crazy when you think about it.

**David:** What would that feedback look like ideally?

**Sarah:** Maybe like... periodic check-ins? Not like surveillance but more like "here's how your grades compare to the average" and "here's where you might be an outlier." Give me information. Let me self-correct. Don't just leave me in the dark.

**David:** That makes a lot of sense.

**Sarah:** Because I want to do a good job! I really do. But I can't improve if no one tells me how I'm doing.

**David:** Thank you Sarah, this has been really helpful.

**Sarah:** Of course! And hey, if you build something that actually shows me how I'm grading compared to everyone else, I'd use that daily. Just saying.

*[laughter, recording ends]*
