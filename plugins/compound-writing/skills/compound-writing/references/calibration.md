# Calibration Sentences

Verbatim sentences from Aman's published writing. Use these as the gold standard for voice matching. Output should feel like it belongs alongside these sentences.

## How to Use This File

When reviewing a draft for voice fidelity:
1. Read each calibration sentence to internalize the voice
2. For each paragraph in the draft, ask: "Could this paragraph appear in the same piece as these sentences?"
3. Flag any paragraph where the answer is no
4. Use the "What Makes It Work" annotations to understand which patterns to apply in rewrites

## Substack Voice

> "The AI couldn't figure out that 45 is less than 60."

*What makes it work:* Short. Devastating. Specific number. Lets the absurdity speak for itself.

> "Without them, you just have a very expensive autocomplete that doesn't know who you are."

*What makes it work:* Deflates complexity casually. "Very expensive autocomplete" makes a technical concept immediately graspable.

> "Their prompt is a changelog of everything they learned about how their AI fails and how to prevent it. Not elegant. Just accumulated wisdom from thousands of real user interactions."

*What makes it work:* Thesis-evidence rhythm. "Not elegant. Just accumulated wisdom" has the punch-then-explain pattern.

> "A $3 trillion company with some of the best engineers in the world built an AI product that worked great in demos and fell apart when they tried to ship it."

*What makes it work:* Leads with concrete specifics ($3 trillion, best engineers). The contrast (demos vs. ship) is earned through the setup, not a false dichotomy.

> "An assistant with no personality is just a search engine with extra steps."

*What makes it work:* Casual reduction of a complex concept. Memorable and quotable.

> "Cool. But also... now what?"

*What makes it work:* Ultra-short. Acknowledges imperfection. The ellipsis creates genuine (not manufactured) pause. This is how a real person transitions.

> "That is literally why I exist. I'm here to try to parse through the occasional PDF or tweet from the Claude team and explain why it matters to you in my own words."

*What makes it work:* Vulnerability + authority. "Literally why I exist" is bold but earned because the next sentence is humble.

> "The difference between 'I use ChatGPT sometimes' and 'I have an AI assistant' is the difference between visiting a library and having a research analyst on staff."

*What makes it work:* Concrete comparison. No buzzwords. The analogy (library vs. research analyst) is vivid and specific.

## O'Reilly Voice

> "If you could build a working AI application prototype in under an hour, would you still wait three sprints for your engineering team to prototype your idea?"

*What makes it work:* Genuine provocative question (not rhetorical setup). Specific time frames create tension.

> "In traditional software development, the distance between an idea and a working product is measured in weeks or months. In the era of AI engineering, it is measured in minutes."

*What makes it work:* Declarative contrast with mirrored structure. Changed variable (weeks/months vs. minutes) is the punch.

> "When prototypes cost weeks of engineering time, only high-confidence ideas get built. When prototypes cost hours of PM time, experimentation becomes the default approach to validation."

*What makes it work:* Same mirrored structure. The insight emerges from the contrast, not from telling the reader what to think.

> "The PMs who develop AI intuition fastest are the ones who start seeing components everywhere: RAG retrieval, reasoning traces, tool calling patterns, context engineering choices. They stop treating AI as a black box and start seeing architecture."

*What makes it work:* Specific list of things to see. The colon introduces concrete examples. The final sentence zooms out to the implication.

## General (Cross-Format)

> "At Cruise, we once deployed a model that scored perfectly on our evals but made passengers carsick with jerky movements. The technical metrics were green, but the human experience was red."

*What makes it work:* Named company, specific failure, hyper-specific detail (carsick, jerky movements). The green/red contrast is earned and visual.

> "I once built twelve different prototypes for an onboarding experience in a single afternoon. None were production-ready; two were functional failures that yielded unexpected learning. But three were sufficient to share with my team by 4:00 PM to discuss."

*What makes it work:* Specific numbers (twelve, two, three, 4:00 PM). Acknowledges imperfection (none production-ready). "By 4:00 PM" makes it real.

> "I'm sure a week after I post this, it will already be out of date."

*What makes it work:* Acknowledges imperfection openly. Builds trust. Self-aware about the pace of the space.
